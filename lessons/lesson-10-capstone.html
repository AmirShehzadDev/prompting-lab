1 · The capstone: the full pipeline

Every technique was a piece of one system. Here's how they snap together into the agent the course promised in Lesson 0.

Messagecustomer→ TicketL6 structured intake→ RouteL2 intent→ Account? → AgentL7 tools/ Else → RAGL8 help center→ ReplyL3 persona

Each incoming message becomes a structured ticket (Lesson 6), whose intent (Lesson 2) decides the path: account-specific questions go to the tool-using agent (Lesson 7) for real data; everything else is answered from the help center via RAG (Lesson 8). The whole thing speaks in the Nimbus persona with guardrails (Lesson 3), and we hold its quality to the eval suite (Lesson 9). Zero-shot (Lesson 1) and chaining (Lesson 5) are the connective tissue.

2 · Wiring it together

The orchestration is small — because the hard work lives in the modules we already built and tested. This is the payoff of one coherent project.

Notice every routing decision is something you learned to build: build_ticket is structured output, the intent check is the router, run_agent is the ReAct loop, rag_answer is retrieval — all wearing the Lesson 3 persona. The persona and guardrails ride along because each underlying call uses NIMBUS_SYSTEM.

One agent, structured intake, the right tool for each request, grounded and on-brand throughout. That is a production-shaped support agent — built from techniques you now understand at the API level and the framework level.

5 · Outdated tricks (and what to do instead)

As promised throughout: a lot of prompting folklore no longer earns its keep on modern instruction-tuned models. Stop spending tokens on these.

TrickVerdictDo instead
"You are a world-renowned expert in…"🟠 Mostly noise (role inflation)A precise role + concrete, testable rules (L3).
"I'll tip you $200" / "my job depends on this"🔴 No reliable effectState the task and constraints clearly.
"Take a deep breath"🟠 Folklore reasoning triggerAsk for steps when needed (L4), or use native thinking.
ALL-CAPS / repeating an instruction 5×🟠 Adds noise, not reliabilityOne clear instruction; enforce shape with a schema (L6).
"Do not hallucinate."🔴 Not a switchGround facts with RAG (L8); constrain with schema (L6).
Elaborate persona / jailbreak framing🔴 Fragile & riskyA real system prompt + code-level guardrails (L3, L6).
Chain-of-thought on every task🟠 Wastes tokens on trivial onesReserve CoT for genuinely multi-step problems (L4).
⚠ Why these faded

Many were real on early models (GPT-3 era) that needed coaxing. Today's instruction-tuned models follow clear directions directly, so incantations mostly add tokens, latency, and noise. The fix for "the model won't behave" is almost never a magic phrase — it's a clearer instruction, an example, a schema, retrieval, or a code guardrail.

6 · Durable vs. outdated: how to tell

You don't have to guess which advice is real. You have a tool for that now.

The difference between a durable technique and a dead trick is simple: does it move your eval? (Lesson 9.) Few-shot, system prompts, CoT-for-hard-tasks, structured output, tools, and RAG all produce measurable lifts on the right tasks. Tips you can't measure a gain from are superstition — pleasant rituals that survive because nobody A/B tested them.

✦ The one meta-skill

Models change fast. What helped last year may be noise today, and today's best practice may age out. The durable skill isn't memorizing techniques — it's the loop: form a hypothesis, build a small eval, measure, keep what wins. That outlives any single model or framework.

7 · Where to go next

This course covered the prompting techniques. Shipping to production adds a few more concerns worth a look:

  • Prompt / context caching — reuse static prefixes (system prompts, few-shot examples, RAG context) to cut cost and latency at scale (touched on in L2/L3).
  • Observability & tracing — log every call, tool, and token; inspect failures. LangSmith or OpenTelemetry-based tracing.
  • Guardrails & safety — input/output moderation, prompt-injection defense, and human approval for irreversible actions (L3 §4, L6 §6).
  • Streaming — stream tokens to the UI so long answers feel fast.
  • Multi-agent systems — specialized agents that hand off (a natural extension of L7 on LangGraph).
  • Prompting vs. fine-tuning — when a task is high-volume and stable, a fine-tuned small model can beat a prompted large one on cost. Prompt first; fine-tune when the eval and the economics say so.
  • Evals in CI — run Lesson 9's suite on every prompt change, like unit tests.
